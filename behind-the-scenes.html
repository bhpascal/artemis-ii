Behind the Scenes

The mistakes we made building this thing

The main page presents orbital mechanics as if we understood it all along. We didn't. This page tells the real story: the bugs, the wrong assumptions, and the moments where the physics was trying to tell us something and we weren't listening.

Mythbusters rules apply. Failure then eventual success teaches more than effortless one-shots.

The Figure-8 That Wouldn't

The classic image of a free-return trajectory is a figure-8: the spacecraft loops out from Earth, swings around the Moon, and comes back in a graceful crossing pattern. In a co-rotating frame — a viewpoint that rotates with the Moon so the Moon stays fixed on the right — this shape emerges naturally.

Our first attempt drew a blob. The departure arc went up and to the right, the flyby arc looped around the Moon, but the return arc came back on the same side, producing a teardrop instead of a figure-8. Three separate fixes were needed before it worked.

Attempt 1: Wrong reference frame

The trajectory was being drawn in the inertial frame — the frame where the Moon moves. In this frame, the figure-8 doesn't exist. The departure arc, flyby arc, and return arc are just three separate ellipses and a hyperbola pointing in different directions. The co-rotating frame rotation was missing entirely.

Fix: Rotate every rendered point by −θMoon, the negative of the Moon's angular position. This pins the Moon to the +x axis and reveals the figure-8.

But it still looked wrong. Teardrop, not figure-8.

Attempt 2: Wrong turn direction

A lunar flyby can bend the trajectory clockwise or counterclockwise, depending on whether the spacecraft passes in front of or behind the Moon. The angular momentum of the approach determines this: L = r × v. Positive L means the spacecraft curves counterclockwise.

We had the sign right in the solver but were applying it inconsistently in the renderer. The flyby arc curved the right way, but the exit position was placed on the wrong side. Result: the return arc departed from the wrong point on the Moon's sphere of influence, and the trajectory crossed itself awkwardly instead of forming a clean loop.

Fix: Propagate the turn sign consistently through both the exit velocity direction and the exit position angle.

Better. But the departure and return arcs still overlapped instead of separating into two lobes.

Attempt 3: Solver and renderer were tangled

This was the real problem. The solver was computing orbital elements and generating render points in the same function. The render points were being generated in a mix of reference frames — some in the perifocal frame, some in the inertial frame, some in the co-rotating frame. The coordinate transformations accumulated errors.

Fix: Rip them apart. The solver became a pure physics module: it takes injection velocity and flyby altitude, returns orbital elements and state vectors. The renderer became a separate module: it takes solver output and a reference frame choice, returns drawable point arrays. With the separation, the coordinate transforms became obvious and the figure-8 appeared immediately.

The 78-Degree Lie

The flyby computation had three physics bugs, and they were textbook bugs — the kind you get from following a textbook too literally.

Standard orbital mechanics texts describe a planetary flyby like this: the spacecraft approaches from infinity with some velocity v∞, the planet's gravity deflects it by a turn angle δ = 2 arcsin(1/e), and it departs toward infinity with the same speed in a new direction. Simple, elegant, and wrong for our case.

Bug 1: Speed at the boundary ≠ speed at infinity

The textbook algorithm assumes you're measuring the spacecraft's velocity infinitely far from the planet. We were measuring it at the Moon's sphere of influence boundary — 66,183 km from the Moon's center. At that distance, the Moon's gravity has already accelerated the spacecraft. The speed at the SOI boundary is about 80 m/s faster than the true v∞.

80 m/s doesn't sound like much when the spacecraft is moving at ~950 m/s relative to the Moon. But it compounds: the wrong speed gives the wrong hyperbolic semi-major axis, the wrong eccentricity, and the wrong turn angle — a cascade of errors from one wrong number.

Fix: Use the vis-viva equation at the actual position: a = 1 / (2/r − v2/μ). This gives the correct orbit from the actual state, no infinity assumptions needed.

Bug 2: The asymptotic turn angle doesn't work at finite distance

The turn angle δ = 2 arcsin(1/e) describes the deflection between the two asymptotes of the hyperbola — the directions the spacecraft would be heading if you extended its path to infinity. At the SOI boundary, the spacecraft is still on the hyperbola, not at infinity. Its actual velocity direction differs from the asymptotic direction.

We were rotating the SOI-boundary velocity by the asymptotic angle. The resulting exit velocity pointed 78 degrees away from where it should have been. The return orbit went completely haywire.

Fix: Don't use the turn angle at all. Instead, compute the eccentricity vector from the actual state at SOI entry. This gives the exact orientation of the hyperbolic orbit. Find the true anomaly at entry, mirror it for exit (symmetric flyby), and compute the exact exit state from the orbital equations. No approximations.

Bug 3: Wrong exit speed

The old code set the exit speed equal to v∞. But the spacecraft is still inside the Moon's gravity well at the SOI boundary, so it should be moving faster than v∞ — the exact same speed it had when it entered (conservation of energy at equal distances from the Moon). The fix for Bug 2 resolved this automatically: computing the exact state at the exit true anomaly gives the correct speed.

The Slider That Lied

After fixing the flyby physics, a new problem appeared: the "flyby altitude" slider didn't actually control the flyby altitude.

In the old (buggy) code, the flyby altitude was a free parameter. You passed it into the solver, and the solver used it to compute the hyperbolic orbit's eccentricity. But with correct physics, the flyby periapsis isn't a free parameter — it's determined by the approach geometry. The spacecraft enters the SOI at some position with some velocity, and those two vectors fully define the hyperbolic orbit, including its closest approach distance. The slider was requesting a periapsis of 8,237 km, but the actual orbit had a periapsis of ~54,000 km.

Fix: Add a targeting loop. The departure orbit's orientation (ω, the argument of periapsis) controls where the spacecraft enters the Moon's SOI, which controls the approach geometry, which controls the flyby periapsis. By scanning ω over a ±15° range and bisecting to find the value that produces the desired periapsis, the slider works again — genuinely this time.

2D Isn't 3D

With all the physics correct, we expected the default parameters — Δv = 3,133 m/s, flyby altitude 6,500 km, matching the real Artemis II mission — to produce a beautiful free-return trajectory ending in a 37 km reentry. The return perigee was 91,325 km. Not even close.

We spent a while hunting for the right analytical solution before realizing: we have the actual Artemis II mission data. The real mission obviously works. We don't need to derive the parameters — we can back-derive them from reality.

A brute-force sweep of the parameter space found the answer: in a 2D patched-conic model, a valid free return at Δv = 3,140 m/s occurs at a flyby altitude of ~10,500 km, not 6,500 km. The return perigee at those parameters: 37 km. Right in the reentry corridor.

Why the difference? The real Artemis II trajectory is three-dimensional. The spacecraft approaches the Moon from below the orbital plane, loops over the far side, and returns above the plane. This out-of-plane component provides additional deflection that a 2D model can't capture. In 2D, you need a wider flyby (more time in the Moon's gravity well) to get the same total deflection.

The lesson: a simplified model isn't wrong, it's simplified. You calibrate it against reality and understand where it diverges. The 2D patched-conic model teaches the same physics as the real trajectory — conservation of energy, gravity assists, the geometry of free returns — it just does it at slightly different parameter values.

What We Learned

Every bug in this project came from the same mistake: applying a formula outside its domain of validity. The textbook flyby equations assume infinite distance; we used them at finite distance. The turn angle describes asymptotic behavior; we applied it at the SOI boundary. The 2D model captures the essential physics; we expected it to reproduce 3D parameter values.

The fixes all had the same shape too: go back to first principles. Don't rotate by the turn angle — propagate the actual orbit. Don't assume v∞ — use vis-viva at the actual position. Don't guess the right parameters — sweep the space and let the math tell you.
